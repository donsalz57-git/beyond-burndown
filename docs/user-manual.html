1. Introduction & Core Concepts

Why Beyond Burndown?

Traditional burndown charts show you what's happenedâ€”how much work is left compared to how much time remains. But they can't tell you whether your plan was realistic in the first place, or whether you'll actually finish on time.

Beyond Burndown takes a fundamentally different approach. Instead of just tracking progress, it analyzes your plan's feasibilityâ€”comparing the work you've committed to against the capacity you have available. This lets you identify problems before they become crises.

The Core Problem

Most project failures aren't caused by teams working too slowly. They're caused by plans that were never realistic to begin with. If you commit to 500 hours of work but only have 400 hours of capacity, no amount of hard work will save you.

Beyond Burndown makes this mismatch visible before you start, so you can adjust scope, add resources, or extend deadlines while there's still time.

The Four Questions

Beyond Burndown is designed to answer four essential questions that every project manager needs to know:

1. Is the plan feasible?

Can we actually deliver this scope by the deadline with the resources we have? The Feasibility Score gives you a clear 0-100% answer. If it's below 80%, you're at risk.

2. Are resources working as planned?

Is the available capacity being applied to the work? Who's overloaded? Who has spare capacity? The Team tab shows you exactly how work is distributed across your team.

3. Is the plan up to date?

Are there overdue issues, missing dates, or missing estimates that need fixing? The Compliance tab automatically detects data quality problems that could throw off your analysis.

4. Is the plan on track?

Is work getting done on schedule? The time spent vs. demand comparison shows whether you're keeping pace with the plan.

Key Concepts

Demand

Demand represents the work that needs to be done. It's calculated from the time estimates on your Jira issues. If an issue has an original estimate of 16 hours, that's 16 hours of demand.

Demand is spread across the issue's date range. If an issue spans 4 business days with 16 hours of estimate, it contributes 4 hours of demand per day.

Capacity

Capacity represents the work that can be doneâ€”the hours your team has available. You can define capacity through manual entry (recommended) or through capacity-representing issues in Jira.

Feasibility

Feasibility is the ratio of capacity to demand. If you have 400 hours of capacity and 500 hours of demand, your feasibility is 80%â€”you can only complete 80% of the planned work.

Time Spent

Time Spent is the actual work logged against issues through Jira's time tracking. This shows whether work is actually getting done as planned.

Overload

An overload occurs when demand exceeds capacity for a given period. Even if your total capacity matches total demand, having some weeks overloaded while others are underutilized causes problems.

2. Getting Started

Installation

  1. Go to the Atlassian Marketplace
  2. Search for "Beyond Burndown"
  3. Click Get it now and select your Jira Cloud site
  4. Confirm the installation and grant the required permissions
Permissions Required

Beyond Burndown needs read access to your Jira issues to analyze them. It also needs storage permission to save your configuration. It does not modify any issuesâ€”it's read-only.

Adding to a Dashboard

  1. Navigate to any Jira dashboard (or create a new one)
  2. Click Add gadget (or the + button in the top right)
  3. In the gadget browser, search for "Beyond Burndown" or "Feasibility"
  4. Click Add gadget to add it to your dashboard
  5. The gadget will appear in edit mode, ready for configuration

Initial Configuration

After adding the gadget, you need to configure it with at least a Demand JQL query:

  1. In the configuration panel, enter a Demand JQL query that returns the issues you want to analyze
  2. Optionally set a Target Date for your deadline (shown as a vertical line on charts)
  3. Click Save to start the analysis

Quick Start Example

For a simple start, try this Demand JQL:

project = YOUR_PROJECT AND sprint in openSprints()

This analyzes all issues in your current active sprints. Replace YOUR_PROJECT with your actual project key.

After saving, go to the Capacity tab to configure your team's capacity. Without capacity configured, the feasibility score will be inaccurate.

3. Feasibility Tab

Understanding Feasibility

The Feasibility tab is the heart of Beyond Burndown. It shows you whether your plan is achievable by comparing capacity (what you can do) against demand (what needs to be done) over time.

Why Cumulative Matters

The key insight is looking at cumulative values, not just daily or weekly snapshots. Even if your average weekly capacity matches average weekly demand, the timing matters enormously.

If all your demand is front-loaded but capacity is spread evenly, you'll fall behind early and may never catch up. The cumulative view makes this visible.

The Feasibility Score

The feasibility score is a 0-100% measure that answers: "What percentage of the planned work can we realistically complete?"

Score Ranges

Score Status What It Means What To Do
80-100% Healthy You have enough capacity with some buffer for uncertainty Plan is realistic. Monitor for changes.
50-79% At Risk Capacity is tight or demand is unevenly distributed Consider reducing scope or adding capacity
0-49% Overloaded Significant capacity shortageâ€”plan will likely fail Immediate action needed: cut scope, add resources, or extend deadline
Why 80% is the Target

We recommend targeting 80% or higher, not 100%. Real projects always have unexpected work, meetings, sick days, and context switching. An 80% score leaves room for reality.

Chart Elements Explained

Period Bars (Capacity vs. Demand)

The bar chart shows capacity and demand for each time period:

  • Green bars â€” Capacity available that period
  • Blue bars â€” Demand (work due) that period

When the blue bar exceeds the green bar, that period is overloadedâ€”you have more work due than capacity available.

Cumulative Lines

The dashed lines show running totals over time:

  • Green dashed line â€” Cumulative Capacity (total hours available so far)
  • Blue dashed line â€” Cumulative Demand (total hours of work needed so far)
  • Orange dashed line â€” Cumulative Time Spent (total hours actually worked)

Completion Percentage

The purple line shows completion percentageâ€”time spent divided by original estimates. This tells you how much of the planned work has actually been completed.

Target Date Line

If you've set a target date, it appears as a red vertical line. This marks your deadline so you can see whether cumulative lines will meet before that date.

How to Read the Chart

Healthy Project

In a healthy project:

  • The green cumulative line (capacity) stays above the blue line (demand)
  • The gap between them represents your buffer
  • The orange line (time spent) tracks close to the blue line, showing work is getting done on schedule

Project at Risk

Warning signs to watch for:

  • The blue line (demand) crosses above the green line (capacity)â€”you're falling behind
  • The orange line (time spent) falls significantly below demandâ€”work isn't getting done
  • The lines converge near or after your target dateâ€”you won't finish on time

Overload Warnings

When demand exceeds capacity for any period, Beyond Burndown displays an overload warning banner showing:

  • Date range â€” When the overload occurs
  • Duration â€” How many days are affected
  • Severity â€” How far over capacity you are

Why Overloads Matter

Overloaded periods create a cascade effect. If you fall behind in week 1, you're starting week 2 already in the hole. This debt compounds unless you have slack later to catch up.

The overload penalty in the feasibility score accounts for thisâ€”even if totals balance, having concentrated overloads hurts your score.

Even when total team capacity matches total demand, individual imbalances cause problems. If Alice has 50 hours of work and Bob has 10 hours, Alice will struggle while Bob waits.

5. Capacity Tab

Understanding Capacity

Capacity represents the work your team can doâ€”the hours available for project work. Getting capacity right is critical for accurate feasibility analysis.

Capacity â‰  Hours in a Day

A common mistake is assuming 8 hours/day of capacity per person. In reality, much of the workday is consumed by:

  • Meetings and standups
  • Email and Slack communication
  • Context switching between tasks
  • Code reviews and helping teammates
  • Administrative tasks

Most teams have 5-6 hours of actual productive capacity per person per day. Be realistic.

Manual Capacity Entry

The Capacity tab lets you configure each team member's availability directly:

  1. Go to the Capacity tab
  2. Choose your period: Weekly or Monthly
  3. Click Add Team Member
  4. Enter their name (should match Jira display names for best results)
  5. Enter their hours per period
  6. Optionally set start/end dates for temporary capacity changes
  7. Click Save Capacity Configuration

Example Configuration

For a typical team:

NameHours/WeekNotes

How Capacity is Calculated

Beyond Burndown converts your configured capacity into daily values:

Period Setting Conversion Example
Weekly Hours Ã· 5 business days 30 hrs/week = 6 hrs/day
Monthly Hours Ã· 22 avg business days 132 hrs/month = 6 hrs/day

These daily values are then aggregated according to your selected period view (daily, weekly, monthly, etc.).

Capacity Best Practices

1. Be Conservative

It's better to underestimate capacity than overestimate. If you assume everyone has 8 productive hours/day and they actually have 5, your feasibility score will be dangerously optimistic.

2. Account for Planned Absences

If someone is taking vacation next week, either reduce their hours or set an end date on their capacity. Don't assume full availability when you know it won't be there.

3. Update Regularly

Capacity changes over time. Update your configuration when team members join, leave, or have schedule changes. Stale capacity data leads to bad analysis.

4. Match Names to Jira

For the Team tab to work correctly, the names you enter should match how they appear as assignees in Jira. Check Jira's "Assigned" field to see the exact display names.

6. Compliance Tab (Data Quality)

Why Data Quality Matters

Feasibility analysis is only as good as the data it's based on. If your issues are missing estimates, have no due dates, or contain conflicting information, the analysis will be misleading.

Garbage In, Garbage Out

The Compliance tab automatically scans your issues and identifies data quality problems. Think of it as a health check for your Jira data.

Fixing these violations doesn't just improve Beyond Burndown's accuracyâ€”it improves your overall project visibility and planning quality.

Violation Types Explained

Missing Start Date

What it means: An issue has no start date, so Beyond Burndown doesn't know when work should begin.

Why it matters: Without a start date, the system assumes work could start immediately. If the issue actually can't start for two weeks, demand will be incorrectly front-loaded.

How to fix: Add a start date to the issue. Use Jira's "Start date" custom field, or the start date of the sprint the issue is in.

Missing Due Date

What it means: An issue has no due date, so there's no clear deadline.

Why it matters: Without a due date, Beyond Burndown uses a default assumption (typically spreading work over a 14-day window). This may not reflect reality.

How to fix: Set a realistic due date that reflects when the work actually needs to be done.

Missing Estimate

What it means: An issue has no time estimate (Original Estimate field is empty).

Why it matters: Issues without estimates contribute zero demand. They're invisible to feasibility analysis. If you have 10 unestimated issues, your true demand is higher than reported.

How to fix: Add an Original Estimate to the issue's time tracking. Even a rough estimate is better than none.

Overdue

What it means: An issue's due date has passed but it's not marked Done.

Why it matters: Overdue issues indicate either planning failures (the deadline was unrealistic) or data maintenance problems (the due date wasn't updated).

How to fix: Either complete the issue, update its due date to a realistic new deadline, or mark it as blocked/deprioritized.

Done with Remaining Work

What it means: An issue is marked Done but still has remaining estimate time.

Why it matters: This usually indicates the issue was closed without proper time logging, or the status is wrong. It skews capacity calculations.

How to fix: Clear the remaining estimate (set to 0), or verify the issue is actually complete.

Child After Parent

What it means: A sub-task or child story has a due date after its parent Epic's due date.

Why it matters: This creates an impossible scheduleâ€”the Epic can't be done until its children are done, but the children are scheduled to finish later.

How to fix: Either move the child's due date earlier, or extend the parent's due date.

Dependency Conflict

What it means: An issue depends on (is blocked by) another issue that finishes after the dependent issue is supposed to start.

Why it matters: You can't start work on a blocked issue until its blocker is done. If the blocker finishes after you're supposed to start, the schedule is impossible.

How to fix: Adjust dates so blockers complete before dependent work begins, or remove the dependency if it's not actually a hard blocker.

Severity Levels

Severity Color Description Priority
Error Red Critical issue that will cause planning failures Fix immediately
Warning Orange Potential problem that may affect accuracy Fix when possible
Info Blue Minor issue or suggestion Nice to fix

How to Fix Violations

The Compliance tab shows each violation with:

  • The issue key (clickable to open in Jira)
  • The violation type and description
  • The severity level
Prioritization Strategy

Start with Errors (red), then Warnings (orange). You don't need to fix everythingâ€”focus on issues that significantly affect your analysis. A few Info-level violations won't throw things off much.

7. Dependencies Tab

Understanding Dependencies

Dependencies represent ordering constraintsâ€”work that must be done in a specific sequence. If Issue A blocks Issue B, then B cannot start until A is complete.

Why Dependencies Matter for Feasibility

Dependencies affect more than just execution order. They can create bottlenecks that reduce effective capacity.

If three issues are all blocked by one issue, that blocker becomes a critical path item. Any delay to it delays everything downstream.

Dependency Analysis

Beyond Burndown analyzes blocking relationships from Jira issue links:

Link Types Recognized

  • Blocks / Is blocked by
  • Has to be done before / Has to be done after
  • Is required by / Requires

Metrics Calculated

Metric Meaning Why It Matters
Dependency Depth How many levels of blockers an issue has (depth 0 = no blockers) High-depth issues are at the end of long chains and are vulnerable to upstream delays
Root Issues Issues with no blockers These can start immediatelyâ€”prioritize them to unblock downstream work
Leaf Issues Issues that don't block anything These are end pointsâ€”their completion doesn't unblock other work

Circular Dependencies

A circular dependency occurs when issues form a cycle: A blocks B, B blocks C, and C blocks A. This is impossible to executeâ€”no issue can start because they're all waiting for each other.

Critical: Break All Cycles

Circular dependencies must be resolved. They're displayed prominently with the full cycle path. You need to remove at least one link to break the cycle.

Often, circular dependencies are created by mistake or represent "nice to have" relationships rather than true blockers.

Critical Path Implications

While Beyond Burndown doesn't calculate critical path directly, the dependency analysis reveals critical path indicators:

  • High-depth chains â€” Long dependency chains have more opportunities for delays
  • Many downstream dependents â€” Issues that block many others are high-risk
  • Root issues on critical path â€” Start these first to unblock downstream work

9. What-If Scenarios

The Power of What-If Analysis

One of Beyond Burndown's most powerful features is the ability to model hypothetical changes and see their impact before committing to them.

Making Informed Decisions

When your feasibility is low, you have three fundamental levers:

  1. Add capacity â€” Hire contractors, approve overtime, reassign people
  2. Reduce scope â€” Cut features, defer work to later releases
  3. Extend timeline â€” Push the deadline out

What-If lets you model each option (or combinations) to find the right balance before making commitments.

Capacity Scenarios

Adding Capacity (+)

Model what happens if you increase resources:

  • +10% â€” Modest increase (overtime, focus time)
  • +25% â€” Significant increase (temporary contractor)
  • +50% â€” Major increase (new team member)
  • Custom â€” Any percentage you want to test

Removing Capacity (-)

Model what happens if you lose resources:

  • -10% â€” Minor reduction (sick days, training)
  • -25% â€” Vacation or extended absence
  • -50% â€” Major reduction (departure, reassignment)

Example: Contractor Decision

Your feasibility is 65% (At Risk). You're considering hiring a contractor.

Try: +25% capacity scenario â†’ Feasibility becomes 81% (Healthy)

Now you have data to support the contractor request.

Scope Scenarios

Removing Scope (-)

Model descoping:

  • -10% â€” Cut nice-to-haves
  • -25% â€” Significant feature reduction
  • -50% â€” Major descope to core MVP

Adding Scope (+)

Model scope creep or new requirements:

  • +10% â€” Minor additions
  • +25% â€” New feature request
  • +50% â€” Major scope expansion

Example: Scope Negotiation

Stakeholder wants to add a feature. Your feasibility is 75%.

Try: +20% scope â†’ Feasibility drops to 58% (At Risk)

Now you can show the impact and negotiate: "We can add this if we cut something else or extend the deadline."

Combining Scenarios

You can activate multiple scenarios simultaneously to find optimal combinations:

Example: Finding the Sweet Spot

  1. Try +25% capacity alone â†’ 58% (still At Risk)
  2. Try -25% scope alone â†’ 60% (still At Risk)
  3. Try both together â†’ 80% (Healthy!)

The combination of a modest capacity increase AND modest scope reduction gets you to a healthy state.

When scenarios are active, the header shows "Viewing X scenarios" and the feasibility score updates in real-time.

10. Configuration

JQL Queries Explained

JQL (Jira Query Language) is how you tell Beyond Burndown which issues to analyze. The Demand JQL query is requiredâ€”it defines what work you're planning.

JQL Basics

JQL uses field = value syntax. Common patterns:

  • project = ABC â€” Issues in project ABC
  • sprint in openSprints() â€” Issues in active sprints
  • assignee = currentUser() â€” Issues assigned to you
  • status != Done â€” Issues not yet completed
  • duedate >= 2026-01-01 â€” Issues due on or after a date

Combine with AND/OR: project = ABC AND sprint in openSprints()

JQL Examples

Current Sprint

project = MYPROJECT AND sprint in openSprints()

Specific Sprint

project = MYPROJECT AND sprint = "Sprint 42"

Date Range (Quarterly Planning)

project = MYPROJECT AND duedate >= 2026-01-01 AND duedate <= 2026-03-31

Exclude Done Issues

project = MYPROJECT AND sprint in openSprints() AND status != Done

Multiple Projects

project in (PROJ1, PROJ2, PROJ3) AND sprint in openSprints()

Using a Saved Filter

filter = "My Release Planning Filter"

or by filter ID:

filter = 12345

Specific Issue Types

project = MYPROJECT AND issuetype in (Story, Task, Bug) AND sprint in openSprints()

Required Issue Fields

For best results, your issues should have these fields populated:

Field Used For If Missing
Original Estimate Demand calculation Issue contributes zero demand (invisible)
Start Date When work begins Assumed to start immediately
Due Date When work must finish 14-day default window applied
Remaining Estimate Work left to do Original estimate used instead
Worklogs Time spent tracking Progress won't show
Assignee Team tab breakdown Shows as "Unassigned"

11. Best Practices

1. Configure Capacity First

Before analyzing feasibility, set up accurate capacity in the Capacity tab. Without capacity, the analysis is meaningless.

2. Use Conservative Estimates

Assume 5-6 productive hours per person per day, not 8. Account for meetings, communication, and context switching.

3. Fix Compliance Violations

Address red (Error) violations first. Missing estimates and impossible schedules will skew your analysis.

4. Review Regularly

Check Beyond Burndown at least weekly. Plans changeâ€”issues get added, estimates change, people take time off. Keep the analysis current.

5. Use What-If for Decisions

Before committing to scope changes or resource decisions, model them in What-If. Let data drive the conversation.

6. Share with Stakeholders

Use the Report tab to generate updates. Stakeholders appreciate data-driven status reports over gut feelings.

7. Target 80% Feasibility

Don't aim for 100%â€”leave buffer for uncertainty. An 80% score means you can handle some surprises.

12. Troubleshooting

No Data Showing

Possible Causes
  • JQL returns no issues â€” Test your query in Jira's issue search first
  • Issues have no estimates â€” Add Original Estimate to your issues
  • Issues have no dates â€” Add Start Date and Due Date
  • Filter permissions â€” If using a saved filter, ensure it's shared appropriately

Feasibility Score is 0%

This means no capacity was detected:

  • Have you configured capacity in the Capacity tab?
  • If using capacity JQL, verify those issues have estimates and dates
  • Try switching to manual capacity entry

Team Tab Shows "No Capacity" for Everyone

The names in your Capacity configuration don't match Jira assignee names. Check:

  • Spelling and capitalization must match exactly
  • Use the display name from Jira, not email addresses
  • Check an assigned issue to see exactly how the name appears

Chart Shows Wrong Date Range

The date range is calculated from your issues:

  • Start = earliest start date (or today if none)
  • End = latest due date + buffer

Check that your issues have reasonable dates. One issue with a due date far in the future will extend the entire chart.

Completion Percentage Stuck at 0%

  • Issues need Original Estimates set (not just Remaining)
  • Time must be logged via Worklogs
  • Worklogs must be within the analysis date range

"Filter Not Found" Error

When using filter = XXXXX:

  • The filter must exist and be accessible
  • Check the filter's sharing settings in Jira
  • Share with "Any logged-in user" or your group

Gadget Loads Slowly

  • Narrow your JQL to fewer issues
  • Add date constraints to limit scope
  • Use saved filters for complex JQL
  • Avoid ORDER BY clauses (not needed)

14. Frequently Asked Questions

Q: Does Beyond Burndown modify my Jira issues?

No. Beyond Burndown is read-only. It analyzes your issues but never creates, modifies, or deletes anything in Jira.

Q: How often does the data refresh?

Data is fetched when you load the dashboard or click refresh. It's not real-timeâ€”you see a snapshot at load time.

Q: Can I use Beyond Burndown for multiple projects?

Yes. Your JQL can span multiple projects: project in (PROJ1, PROJ2, PROJ3). You can also add multiple gadgets to a dashboard with different configurations.

Q: Why is my feasibility score different from what I calculated manually?

Beyond Burndown applies an overload penaltyâ€”even if total capacity equals total demand, having concentrated overload periods reduces the score. This reflects the reality that uneven distribution causes problems.

Q: What if my team doesn't use time estimates in Jira?

Beyond Burndown relies on estimates for demand calculation. Without estimates, issues contribute zero demand. Consider adding at least rough estimates, or use story points converted to hours.

Q: Can I export the charts?

Currently, you can export the status report text. For charts, use your browser's screenshot functionality or a screen capture tool.

Q: Does it work with Jira Server/Data Center?

No. Beyond Burndown is built on Atlassian Forge, which only supports Jira Cloud.

Q: How do I handle team members who work on multiple projects?

In the Capacity tab, enter their total available capacity. Their demand will be calculated from whatever issues are assigned to them within your JQL scope.

Q: What's the difference between Load % and Utilization %?

Load % = Demand Ã· Capacity (how much work is planned vs. available time)

Utilization % = Time Spent Ã· Capacity (how much work is actually being done vs. available time)

High load with low utilization might indicate blockers or procrastination. Low load suggests spare capacity.
